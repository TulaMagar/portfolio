Running







Running can significantly improve physical and mental health. As a form of aerobic exercise, running can reduce stress, improve heart health, and even help alleviate symptoms of depression. Some researchers think running may be so good for us because it's something we evolved to do.



Benefits of running are same as walking but in some places running is better choice. Running isn't a very simple physical activity. Running will help you to improve health. Some of running benefits are as follow;

  • Weight Loss

    Running burn your calories very quick. If you want to loss weight then running is the best way to loss fat faster.

    Gaining weight increases your risk of major health problems. People who are overweight or obese (BMI 30 or more) are more likely to have heart disease, strokes, diabetes, cancer, and depression.

  • Food Digestion

    More research has found that running helps to digest food faster. This could help improve satiety after eating. There's also evidence that links this type of faster digestion with lower rates of heartburn and other reflux symptoms

    Stomach disturbances like gas, bloating, constipation, diarrhea, and heartburn can all be signs of an unhealthy gut. A balanced gut will have less difficulty processing food and eliminating waste.

  • Burn Calories

    Walking can provide a lot of the same benefits of running. But running burns nearly double the number of calories as walking. For example, for someone who's 160 pounds, running at 5 miles per hour (mph) burns 606 calories. Walking briskly for the same amount of time at 3.5 mph burns just 314 calories.

    Gaining weight increases your risk of major health problems. People who are overweight or obese (BMI 30 or more) are more likely to have heart disease, strokes, diabetes, cancer, and depression.

  • Improves Sleep

    For example, being exposed to natural light by running outside in the morning helps entrain a strong circadian rhythm, which promotes healthy sleep. Exercise is also associated with stress reduction and physical well-being, both of which could be pathways to sleeping better.

    Some of the most serious potential problems associated with chronic sleep deprivation are high blood pressure, diabetes, heart attack, heart failure or stroke. Other potential problems include obesity, depression, impairment in immunity and lower sex drive. Chronic sleep deprivation can even affect your appearance.

  • Bone Strength

    Bone is living tissue and becomes stronger with exercise. Runnig involves your feet and legs supporting your weight so that your bones have to work harder and this makes them stronger.

    A person may have low bone mass at any age but not develop osteoporosis. However, if a person has low bone mass and continues to lose bone density, this may lead to osteoporosis. A combination of low bone mass and a risk factor for fracture may increase your risk for broken bones, too.
